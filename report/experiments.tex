\section{Experiments}
\label{sec:experiments}

\subsection{Research Questions}
\label{sec:rqs}

We ask how strongly a single PPO learner embedded in a five-nation
grand-strategy environment is shaped by (i) the opponent ecosystem it trains
against and (ii) the structure of its reward signal, and whether those choices
trade off narrow specialization against cross-ecosystem generalization. We
decompose this into four sub-questions:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{RQ1 (Specialization).} Does a policy trained against a
          homogeneous opponent pool exploit that pool, and how hard does it
          fall over on other pools?
    \item \textbf{RQ2 (Reward shaping).} Given a fixed opponent pool, how does
          reward design change the \emph{kind} of strategy the learner
          converges to, not just its win rate?
    \item \textbf{RQ3 (Behavior vs.\ scoreboard).} Can two policies with
          comparable win rates follow qualitatively different action
          distributions?
    \item \textbf{RQ4 (Seed variance).} How much of the observed behavior is
          signal rather than seed noise at $200$k training steps?
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

A single PPO learner controls one nation out of five. It observes a
$196$-dimensional symbolic encoding and selects from a $120$-action discrete
catalog gated by an action mask, so illegal actions (e.g.\ declaring war
during the $25$-turn grace period) are never sampled. We use the
\texttt{MaskablePPO} implementation from \texttt{sb3-contrib} with a two-layer
MLP policy ($256$ hidden units), $n_{\text{steps}}{=}2048$, batch size $256$,
$\gamma{=}0.99$, GAE $\lambda{=}0.95$, clip range $0.2$, entropy coefficient
$0.01$, learning rate $3{\times}10^{-4}$, and $200$k environment steps per
run (default horizon $T_{\max}{=}100$ turns). All four opponents are
rule-based; no learning opponents or LLM agents are used, isolating the effect
of opponent \emph{strategy} from opponent \emph{capability}.

\subsection{Rule-Based Opponent Library}
\label{sec:opponents}

We extend a single heuristic agent into six profiles by overriding a small
set of decision thresholds (war turn, manpower / production gates, invest
threshold, harvest priority, diplomacy skip flags). Each profile realizes a
distinct theory of how to win.

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lp{11.5cm}}
\toprule
\textbf{Strategy} & \textbf{Key Behaviors} \\
\midrule
\textbf{balanced}   & Baseline heuristic; runs all five decision phases unchanged. \\
\textbf{aggressor}  & Skips diplomacy, declares war as early as turn $20$, drops the military-tech prerequisite, biases harvests toward manpower / production. \\
\textbf{turtle}     & Never attacks, accepts every alliance, invests aggressively at low gold thresholds. \\
\textbf{scientist}  & Skips military, prioritizes research and civic harvests, double-passes research-pact proposals. \\
\textbf{trader}     & Skips military, double-passes trade pacts, channels surplus gold into permanent yield boosts. \\
\textbf{diplomat}   & Skips military, proposes and accepts every alliance, aims for peace victory. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Four of these (\texttt{aggressor}, \texttt{turtle}, \texttt{scientist},
\texttt{diplomat}) are used to build opponent pools; \texttt{balanced}
doubles as an ecosystem and an evaluation suite; \texttt{trader} is kept
in the library but unused here.

\subsection{Three-Axis Experimental Matrix}
\label{sec:matrix}

We sweep three orthogonal axes.

\paragraph{Axis A: opponent composition.}
Four fixed opponent pools:
\textsc{homo-balanced} (four \texttt{balanced}),
\textsc{homo-aggressor} (four \texttt{aggressor}),
\textsc{mixed-diverse} (one each of
\texttt{aggressor}, \texttt{turtle}, \texttt{scientist}, \texttt{diplomat}),
\textsc{homo-diplomat} (four \texttt{diplomat}). These four pools double as
evaluation suites (Section~\ref{sec:eval}).

\paragraph{Axis B: reward shaping.}
Five variants sweeping the dense scale and terminal / annex bonus structure
(Table~\ref{tab:rewards}).

\begin{table}[h]
\centering
\small
\begin{tabular}{lrrrrr}
\toprule
Variant & $\alpha_{\text{dense}}$ & $r_{\text{win}}$ & $r_{\text{peace}}$ &
$r_{\text{loss}}$ & $r_{\text{annex}}$ \\
\midrule
\textsc{baseline}      & $0.01$ & $100$ & $50$  & $-100$ & $20$  \\
\textsc{dense-only}    & $0.05$ & $0$   & $0$   & $0$    & $0$   \\
\textsc{terminal-only} & $0.00$ & $100$ & $50$  & $-100$ & $0$   \\
\textsc{annex-heavy}   & $0.01$ & $100$ & $0$   & $-100$ & $100$ \\
\textsc{peace-heavy}   & $0.01$ & $50$  & $200$ & $-100$ & $0$   \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\caption{Reward variants. $\alpha_{\text{dense}}$ scales per-turn score delta;
terminal bonuses fire at episode end; $r_{\text{annex}}$ fires per opponent
annexation.}
\label{tab:rewards}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Axis C: training horizon.}
$T_{\max}{=}100$ by default, plus two runs at $T_{\max}{=}200$ with a
proportionally larger training budget ($320$k vs.\ $200$k steps) to probe
whether longer horizons induce qualitatively different strategies.

\paragraph{Run schedule.}
Rather than the full $4{\times}5{\times}2$ product, we use a budget-aware
design totaling $18$ runs:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Main grid (12):} four opponent pools $\times$ three reward
          variants (\textsc{baseline}, \textsc{annex-heavy}, \textsc{peace-heavy}),
          $T_{\max}{=}100$.
    \item \textbf{Reward ablation (2):} \textsc{dense-only} and
          \textsc{terminal-only} against \textsc{mixed-diverse}, completing
          the five-way reward sweep at the headline cell.
    \item \textbf{Seed variance (2):} two additional seeds of
          \textsc{baseline}+\textsc{mixed-diverse}.
    \item \textbf{Long horizon (2):} \textsc{baseline} and \textsc{annex-heavy}
          against \textsc{mixed-diverse} at $T_{\max}{=}200$.
\end{itemize}

All runs share the same network, optimizer, and learner index ($0$), and are
dispatched in parallel across four GPUs.

\subsection{Evaluation Protocol}
\label{sec:eval}

Every trained policy is evaluated against the same battery of four suites:
\textsc{vs-balanced}, \textsc{vs-aggressor}, \textsc{vs-diverse},
\textsc{vs-diplomat}. For each suite we run $20$ deterministic episodes
(greedy policy, action masking, fixed seed schedule). Evaluation suites are
decoupled from training pools, so a policy trained on one ecosystem is
tested on all four --- directly exposing overfitting (RQ1). For the strategy
fingerprint (Section~\ref{sec:rq2}) we additionally play $5$ deterministic
episodes per (policy, suite) cell and tally the chosen actions into four
semantic categories: \textit{aggression} (war, strike, skirmish, sabotage,
military invest), \textit{science} (research, civics, science / civics
harvest, research pact), \textit{economy} (gold / production harvest,
industry / manpower invest, trade), and \textit{diplomacy} (alliance, trade,
research-pact proposals / accepts).

\paragraph{(i) The \textsc{vs-aggressor} column is a wall.}
No policy exceeds $0.20$ on \textsc{vs-aggressor} and the column mean is
$0.065$. Four simultaneous \texttt{aggressor}s who declare war as early as
turn $20$ simply overwhelm a single learner; even training on aggressors does
not help (\textsc{base}$_{\text{agg}}$ matches the $0.20$ global ceiling).
The strong form of RQ1 (``training on $X$ helps you beat $X$'') is
\emph{falsified} for the \texttt{aggressor} ecosystem.

\paragraph{(ii) Specialization splits the remaining policies into three bands.}
\begin{itemize}
    \item \emph{Soft-suite specialists} (\textsc{annex}$_{\text{bal}}$,
          \textsc{base}$_{\text{dip}}$, \textsc{peace}$_{\text{dip}}$): score
          $1.00$ on \textsc{vs-balanced} and \textsc{vs-diplomat} but $0.00$
          on \textsc{vs-diverse}. The exploit transfers between the two
          passive suites but breaks the moment one \texttt{aggressor} or
          \texttt{turtle} appears.
    \item \emph{Diverse specialists} (all three \textsc{base}$_{\text{div}}$
          seeds, \textsc{peace}$_{\text{div}}$): $0.75$--$0.95$ on
          \textsc{vs-diverse}, $1.00$ on \textsc{vs-diplomat}, but $0.00$ on
          \textsc{vs-balanced}. The policies expect one specific
          aggressor-shaped neighbor that never appears in a homogeneous
          balanced pool.
    \item \emph{Cross-suite survivors}
          (\textsc{dense-only}$_{\text{div}}$, \textsc{peace}$_{\text{bal}}$,
          \textsc{annex}$_{\text{div}}$, \textsc{base}$_{\text{agg}}$): the
          only runs with $\bar{w}>0.55$. Their training opponents were either
          diverse or aggressive --- i.e.\ no single exploit existed.
\end{itemize}

\paragraph{(iii) Diversity beats difficulty.}
The usual advice --- train against the hardest opponent --- fails here.
\textsc{base}$_{\text{agg}}$ is the best \textsc{vs-aggressor} performer but
is beaten on $\bar{w}$ by three runs that never saw aggressors, including the
winner \textsc{dense-only}$_{\text{div}}$. Against an asymmetric rule-based
library, ecosystem \emph{diversity} predicts generalization better than
ecosystem \emph{difficulty}.

\subsubsection{RQ2 --- Reward shaping rewrites the policy, not just its score}
\label{sec:rq2}

Fig.~\ref{fig:fingerprint_full} reports the action-category fingerprint for
every (policy, suite) cell.

\begin{figure}[!htbp]
\centering
\includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{figures/fingerprint_grid_pub.pdf}
\caption{Full $18{\times}4$ fingerprint. Each policy occupies a group of four
bars, one per evaluation suite. Tick colors encode reward family. Policies
whose bars shift across suites are \emph{reactive}; those whose bars are
identical across suites are \emph{rigid}.}
\label{fig:fingerprint_full}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{(i) \textsc{annex-heavy} is the \emph{most science-dominated} family.}
Every \textsc{annex-heavy} policy runs $0.82$--$0.93$ of its actions through
\texttt{RESEARCH}, \texttt{PURSUE\_CIVIC}, and science harvests on
\textsc{vs-aggressor} and \textsc{vs-diplomat}; aggression stays at
$0.01$--$0.25$. Within this environment, annexations are obtained by
tech-rushing to a military unlock, and PPO collapses onto that indirect
route. The reward \emph{names} the target outcome; the policy finds the
cheapest causal route, and the cheapest route is not the lexically obvious
one.

\paragraph{(ii) \textsc{peace-heavy} against diplomats becomes a pure aggressor.}
\textsc{peace}$_{\text{dip}}$ runs $0.61$--$0.82$ aggression on every
evaluation suite --- higher than any \textsc{annex-heavy} row. Four
\texttt{diplomat} opponents never retaliate, so the $r_{\text{peace}}{=}200$
bonus is attainable by killing everyone cheaply and then settling down. The
reward meant to encourage diplomacy has produced its own bloodless-conquest
exploit. The win-rate row ($1.00/0.05/0.00/1.00$) shows the exploit is
completely non-transferable.

\paragraph{(iii) Against \textsc{homo-aggressor}, everyone runs to science.}
$16$ of $18$ policies allocate $64$--$93\%$ of their actions to science on
\textsc{vs-aggressor}. This is not learned preference but masked behavior:
once war breaks out and resources drain, the action mask eliminates most
economy / diplomacy options, and science remains the dominant legal choice.
The $0.065$ average win rate is consistent --- the learner loses not because
it chose wrong but because its only remaining legal actions cannot matter
in time.

\paragraph{(iv) \textsc{dense-only} has the flattest fingerprint and the best $\bar{w}$.}
\textsc{dense-only}$_{\text{div}}$ ($\alpha_{\text{dense}}{=}0.05$, no
terminal / annex bonuses) attains $\bar{w}{=}0.68$ --- the highest in the
matrix --- with a roughly $50$/$50$ science / economy mix on
\textsc{vs-balanced} and no collapse on the other three suites.
\textsc{terminal-only}$_{\text{div}}$ sits at the opposite extreme with
$\bar{w}{=}0.05$, cleanly bracketing the reward axis: \emph{removing}
structurally exploitable terminal bonuses produced the most generalizable
policy in the study.

\subsubsection{RQ3 --- Win-rate ties hide strategy disparity}
\label{sec:rq3}

Three policies achieve exactly $1.00/0.10/0.00/1.00$ on the heatmap:
\textsc{annex}$_{\text{bal}}$, \textsc{base}$_{\text{dip}}$, and
\textsc{peace}$_{\text{dip}}$. Their fingerprints disagree completely ---
\textsc{annex}$_{\text{bal}}$ is science-leaning ($0.43$--$0.87$ science),
\textsc{base}$_{\text{dip}}$ is strongly aggressive ($0.26$--$0.89$
aggression on three suites), and \textsc{peace}$_{\text{dip}}$ is
aggression-dominated across \emph{all} four suites. Three different reward
families, three different training pools, and one indistinguishable heatmap
row, yet three distinct theories of the game. The generalization heatmap and
the action fingerprint carry independent information, and evaluation
protocols that track only win rate will mislabel qualitatively different
learners as equivalent.

\subsubsection{RQ4 --- Seed variance is directional, not magnitudinal}
\label{sec:rq4}

The three \textsc{baseline}+\textsc{mixed-diverse} seeds yield:

\begin{center}
\small
\begin{tabular}{lcccc}
\toprule
Seed & vs-bal & vs-agg & vs-div & vs-dip \\
\midrule
seed $0$ & $0.00$ & $0.10$ & $0.50$ & $1.00$ \\
seed $1$ & $0.00$ & $0.10$ & $0.95$ & $1.00$ \\
seed $2$ & $0.00$ & $0.00$ & $0.75$ & $1.00$ \\
\midrule
mean     & $0.00$ & $0.07$ & $\mathbf{0.73}$ & $1.00$ \\
s.d.     & $0.00$ & $0.06$ & $\mathbf{0.23}$ & $0.00$ \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabular}
\end{center}

Three of four columns are essentially noise-free ($\sigma{\leq}0.06$): the
overfitting pattern (collapse on \textsc{vs-balanced} / \textsc{vs-aggressor},
sweep on \textsc{vs-diplomat}) is seed-stable. The fourth column ---
\textsc{vs-diverse}, the suite that matches training --- spans
$0.50$--$0.95$ ($\sigma{=}0.23$), telling us how much headroom PPO had left
at $200$k steps. Qualitative claims in this paper are seed-robust, but the
absolute win rate on the training suite should be read as $0.7 \pm 0.2$,
not a point estimate.

\subsubsection{Axis C --- Long-horizon runs}
\label{sec:rq5}

$T_{\max}{=}200$ does not produce qualitatively new behavior: the two runs
track their $T_{\max}{=}100$ counterparts in both fingerprint and win-rate
pattern ($\bar{w}{=}0.39$ and $0.12$). The long horizon does appear to hurt
\textsc{annex}$_{\text{div}}$ on \textsc{vs-diplomat} ($1.00{\to}0.20$), as
diplomats gain more turns to build alliances that block late-game annexation.
With only two runs on this axis, we flag this as a hint rather than a claim,
and defer a proper sweep to the extended matrix.

\subsection{Headline Takeaways}
\label{sec:summary}

\begin{enumerate}
    \item A specific rule-based ecosystem can be unwinnable regardless of
          reward or training distribution: four homogeneous
          \texttt{aggressor}s cap every policy in our sweep at
          $w{\leq}0.20$ (RQ1).
    \item Ecosystem diversity beats ecosystem difficulty for training. The
          best policy by $\bar{w}$ is \textsc{dense-only}$_{\text{div}}$
          ($\bar{w}{=}0.68$), and it never saw aggressors (RQ1).
    \item Reward shaping rewrites the action distribution in counter-intuitive
          ways: \textsc{annex-heavy} produces the most science-oriented
          policies, \textsc{peace-heavy} vs.\ diplomats produces the most
          aggressive policy in the matrix. PPO finds the cheapest causal
          route to the named reward, not the lexical one (RQ2).
    \item Three heatmap-identical policies have three disjoint fingerprints
          --- win-rate-only evaluation mislabels different learners as
          equivalent (RQ3).
    \item Seeds agree on direction, disagree on magnitude. We report bands,
          not point estimates (RQ4).
    \item The clearest positive design lesson: for transfer across rule-based
          ecosystems, remove structurally exploitable terminal / annex bonuses
          and let the per-turn score delta be the gradient signal.
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Reproducibility}
\label{sec:repro}

The full matrix is declaratively specified in a single YAML file
(\texttt{experiments/matrix.yaml}). \texttt{run\_all\_train.sh} fans out one
\texttt{train\_ppo.py} subprocess per run with GPU round-robin assignment and
caps per-worker BLAS threads. Each run writes its checkpoint, four
intermediate checkpoints, training-curve CSV, resolved hyperparameter JSON,
and per-run log into its own directory. \texttt{run\_all\_eval.sh} then
consumes those directories and runs the evaluation battery and visualization
passes end-to-end. Both scripts support \texttt{--only} for re-running a
subset and \texttt{--skip-existing} for resuming a partial sweep. All numbers
in Section~\ref{sec:results} are reproduced by:

\begin{verbatim}
./run_all_train.sh --matrix experiments/matrix.yaml
./run_all_eval.sh  --exp outputs/exp_main --episodes 20
python experiments/plot_fingerprint_full.py  outputs/exp_main --episodes 5
python experiments/plot_training_curves_pub.py outputs/exp_main
python experiments/plot_heatmap_pub.py         outputs/exp_main
python experiments/plot_fingerprint_pub.py     outputs/exp_main
\end{verbatim}
